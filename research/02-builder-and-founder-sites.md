# Builder & Founder Personal Site Inspiration

I now have comprehensive research to craft a detailed, actionable answer. Let me compile it into a well-structured response.

Here's a curated breakdown of 10 personal websites from product managers, founders, and technical builders that strongly align with your goal of projecting **trustworthiness, reliability, and approachable friendliness**, followed by a strategic analysis of what you can steal for your own surdu.de.

***

## Your Current Site: Context First

Your existing surdu.de is clean and factual, an experience timeline that reads like a LinkedIn profile. It establishes competence (Senior PM, InnoGames, millions of users), but it doesn't yet invite collaboration, tell *why* someone should reach out to you, or communicate personality. The sites below fix exactly that.

***

## 10 Sites Worth Studying

### 1. Lee Robinson, leerob.com

Lee is VP of Developer Education at Cursor (formerly Vercel) with a strongly technical background. His site is ultra-minimal: one paragraph about who he is (developer, writer, husband, father, music fan), a short list of favorite articles, and links to his work. The radical brevity makes it feel like talking to a real human, not reading a resume. He mixes technical credibility ("coding for 15 years") with personal warmth ("massive music fan, last listened to..."), which is exactly the trust+approachability combo you're after.

**What to steal:** A short, first-person opening paragraph that mixes professional identity with something genuinely personal (your Ironman finishes work great here).

***

### 2. Guillermo Rauch, rauchg.com

Guillermo is the founder/CEO of Vercel and creator of Next.js. His site is ruthlessly clean, excellent typography, a dark/light mode toggle, and a chronological table of blog posts with years as separators. The About page narrates his journey from Buenos Aires teenager advocating Linux to building tools that power Nintendo and Porsche. It reads like a conversation, not a CV. As a technical founder, his site says "I earned this with my hands" without being arrogant.

**What to steal:** A narrative About page that traces your origin from Game Developer to Team Lead to Product Lead to Senior PM, framed as a learning journey rather than a job list.

***

### 3. Arvid Kahl, thebootstrappedfounder.com

Arvid is a developer-turned-founder who co-built, bootstrapped, and sold FeedbackPanda (a SaaS for online teachers) in under two years, then became a full-time creator. His site opens with *"Hi! I'm Arvid. I empower founders & creators who want to build calm & sustainable businesses in public."*, one sentence, no jargon, immediate value proposition. The About page then lists every podcast he's appeared on, building social proof in a humble, transparent way. He's the blueprint for a technical founder positioning himself as a reliable collaborator.

**What to steal:** A clear one-line personal positioning statement right in the hero. "I help founders build products that actually work" beats any timeline.

***

### 4. Derek Sivers, sive.rs

Derek sold CD Baby for $22M and is now a minimalist author/programmer. His site is literally hand-coded HTML, no frameworks, no libraries, and it's famous in developer circles for that. The About page is a long-form, deeply personal essay covering his philosophy, work ethic, and values. It's trust-building through radical authenticity: you feel like you *know* this person after reading it. He also has a `/now` page showing what he's currently focused on, which is a low-effort but high-trust signal.

**What to steal:** A `/now` page showing your current projects, what you're building, and what kind of work you want. This is enormously useful for potential collaborators scanning your site.

***

### 5. Swyx (Shawn Wang), swyx.io

Swyx is a developer/founder/angel investor prominent in the AI engineering community, founder of Smol.ai, and editor of Latent.Space. His site opens with: *"Writer, Founder, Devtools Startup Advisor. I foster the Rise of the AI Engineer."*, an immediate, opinionated positioning. It then shows his latest writing (essays, podcast episodes, videos) with emoji-coded content types, making his consistent output visible and trustworthy. The /about page has a "README about me" format, treating himself like an open-source project, which resonates deeply with technical audiences.

**What to steal:** A content feed (even a simple list of recent articles or projects) that proves you're actively building and thinking, not just listing past jobs.

***

### 6. Jon Yongfook, yongfook.com

Jon is the founder of Bannerbear (image/video generation API, bootstrapped to $77K MRR) and a prolific open startup sharer. His site is almost comically simple: "Hello I'm Jon Yongfook. I'm the founder of Bannerbear." with clear links to his business and personal blog. What makes it work is the honesty of the open startup model: numbers shared publicly build radical trust. He positions himself as *approachable* through simplicity and *reliable* through consistent public building.

**What to steal:** A "what I'm building" section with links to current projects, even small ones. Showing active work signals you're someone worth partnering with.

***

### 7. Pieter Levels, levels.io

Pieter is the legendary indie maker behind Nomad List and RemoteOK ($3M+ ARR). His site famously uses minimal, hand-coded HTML/CSS, a deliberate aesthetic choice that signals "I ship fast and focus on what matters." His `/projects` page lists every project he's ever built, which signals prolific reliability. He's not trying to look corporate, that approachability is a key part of why founders want to collaborate with or learn from him.

**What to steal:** A simple projects page that shows the breadth of what you've touched, game dev, tech lead, PM, and any side explorations. Technical breadth is a collaboration magnet.

***

### 8. Simon Pan, simonpan.com

Simon is a Senior Staff Designer at Google, known for detailed UX case studies (Uber Magic 2.0, Amazon Prime Music) that are described as "the gold standard of what hiring managers look for". His site leads with deep case studies that narrate the *process* behind decisions, showing how he thinks, not just what he shipped. For a technical PM background, this matters: founders hiring collaborators want to understand your decision-making framework.

**What to steal:** 1-2 short case studies from InnoGames: what was the problem, how did you think about it, what did you ship, and what was the result? Even 300 words per case study is transformative.

***

### 9. Danny Postma, dannypostma.com

Danny is the Dutch founder of HeadshotPro (AI headshots that hit $300K MRR) and a prolific builder-in-public. His site is direct and personality-forward, he documents his journey openly, including failures, which is the foundation of trust. His personal brand is "I build things and show you how", immediately useful to any potential collaborator or partner.

**What to steal:** A short "how I work" or "what I believe" section. Even 5 bullet points about your working style, values, or what excites you makes you feel like a real person to reach out to.

***

### 10. Julian Shapiro, julian.com

Julian is the founder of Demand Curve, former VP Marketing at Webflow, and creator of the animation library Velocity.js. His site is centered entirely on writing, no flashy design, just his essays presented as a newsletter gateway. He treats his personal site as a platform for his *ideas*, not just his credentials. The lesson: if you have opinions about product, SaaS, or building, publishing even a few essays positions you as a thought partner rather than just a service provider.

**What to steal:** A simple writing section or link to where you publish your thoughts (could be a newsletter, blog, or even curated LinkedIn posts).

***

## Applying This to surdu.de

| What you have | What to add |
|---|---|
| Experience timeline (job history) | First-person hero paragraph with personality + positioning statement |
| No clear "why reach out" | `/now` page + CTA: "Open to advising / co-building / consulting" |
| No projects section | Short list of things you've built or shipped (including side projects) |
| No social proof | 2-3 sentences from people you've worked with, or metrics from InnoGames work |
| No personality | Mention Ironman, trail running, AI enthusiasm, founders hire *people* |
| No writing | Even 3 short articles establishes you as a thought partner |

The single most effective upgrade from all 10 sites above: **write 2-3 sentences explaining exactly what kind of person you want to attract and what you can help them with.** Arvid Kahl, Lee Robinson, and swyx all open with this, and it transforms a portfolio into an invitation.
